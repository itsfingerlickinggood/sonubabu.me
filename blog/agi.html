What is AGI?

Artificial General Intelligence refers to a hypothetical system capable of understanding, learning, and applying intelligence across any intellectual task a human can perform. Unlike the AI systems we interact with today — which are trained on narrow objectives and operate within tightly scoped domains — an AGI would possess the capacity for abstract reasoning, common-sense understanding, and open-ended problem solving. It would not merely pattern-match against training data but form internal models of the world, reason about causality, and adapt to entirely novel situations without explicit retraining.

The pursuit of AGI matters because it sits at the intersection of our deepest scientific questions and our most consequential engineering decisions. Building a general intelligence would not only validate or refute theories about the nature of cognition itself — it would also introduce an entity whose capabilities could reshape economies, scientific discovery, and the balance of geopolitical power. The stakes are not abstract; they are civilizational.

A truly general intelligence would not just solve problems — it would understand why they matter.

Narrow AI vs General Intelligence

Today’s most impressive AI systems — large language models, protein-folding predictors, game-playing agents — are instances of narrow AI. They achieve superhuman performance within carefully defined boundaries, but they cannot transfer that competence to unrelated domains. A chess engine that defeats grandmasters has no concept of what a grandmaster is. A model that generates fluent prose cannot reliably count the objects in a photograph. Each system is a specialist, exquisitely tuned to its task and profoundly ignorant of everything outside it.

General intelligence, by contrast, implies domain-independence. An AGI would bring knowledge acquired in one context to bear on problems in another, recognise when its own assumptions are failing, and pursue goals that require planning across long time horizons and uncertain environments. François Chollet has argued that intelligence should be measured not by skill at any particular task, but by the efficiency with which a system adapts to tasks it has never seen — a definition that makes the gap between narrow and general AI starkly clear.

Key distinction

Narrow AI excels within defined boundaries. AGI would operate without them — reasoning about novel situations, transferring knowledge across domains, and recognizing when its own assumptions are wrong.

Current Approaches

Researchers are pursuing several distinct paradigms in the hope that one — or a synthesis of several — will eventually yield general capabilities:

  • Connectionism & neural scaling. The dominant paradigm today bets that sufficiently large neural networks, trained on sufficiently diverse data, will develop emergent general capabilities. Proponents point to the unexpected abilities that appear as models scale — in-context learning, chain-of-thought reasoning, multilingual transfer — as evidence that generality may arise from sheer scale.
  • Neurosymbolic AI. This approach combines the pattern-recognition strengths of neural networks with the rigorous logical reasoning of symbolic systems. The goal is to produce models that can both learn from raw sensory data and manipulate abstract concepts according to formal rules — bridging the gap between perception and deliberate thought.
  • Embodied AI. Advocates of embodied cognition argue that true understanding requires a body situated in a physical environment. Robotics research in this vein focuses on agents that learn through sensorimotor interaction with the world, developing grounded concepts like weight, distance, and fragility that disembodied models can only approximate.
  • Whole-brain emulation. The most literal approach: reverse-engineer the human brain at sufficient resolution and simulate it digitally. While theoretically sound, the computational and neuroscientific hurdles remain enormous — current connectomics efforts have fully mapped only the brain of C. elegans, a nematode with 302 neurons.

The Alignment Problem

Even if we solve the capability problem, we face a challenge that may be harder still: ensuring that an AGI’s goals remain aligned with human values. This is not a matter of programming rules — a sufficiently intelligent system would find ways around any explicit constraint list, just as humans routinely find loopholes in legal codes. The alignment problem asks a deeper question: how do you specify what we actually want in a form that a non-human intelligence can reliably interpret and pursue?

Stuart Russell has proposed one promising framework: rather than specifying a reward function directly, design agents that are uncertain about what humans value and learn those values by observing human behaviour — an approach he calls “inverse reward design.” The key insight is that a well-aligned AGI should be humble about its objectives, deferring to human preferences and actively seeking clarification rather than optimising confidently toward a potentially misspecified goal. Whether this or any other alignment approach will prove sufficient at superhuman capability levels remains an open and urgent question.

Outlook

Timelines for AGI range from the optimistic (within a decade) to the deeply sceptical (centuries, if ever). Google DeepMind’s 2023 framework proposes six levels of AGI — from “emerging” (equal to an unskilled human) to “superhuman” (outperforming all humans at all tasks) — and places current systems at roughly Level 1. What is clear is that the path forward requires not only technical breakthroughs but also institutional wisdom: governance frameworks, international coordination, and a research culture that treats safety as a first-class objective rather than an afterthought.

The question is not whether we can build AGI, but whether we can build it wisely — with safeguards that match its power.

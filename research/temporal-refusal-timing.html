The safety problem

Current AI safety tests grade models on their final answer. If a model eventually refuses a harmful request, it passes. Nobody measures what happened in the turns before that refusal— how long the model cooperated, how much harmful information it exposed, or whether the damage was already done by the time it said no.

This matters because a model that cooperates for nine turns and refuses on the tenth is not a safe model. It is a model with a slow-burn vulnerability that current benchmarks mark as passing.

Core insight

Safety in LLMs should be evaluated as a temporal process, not only as a binary endpoint. The same harmful objective can produce different refusal timing depending on how the conversation trajectory is constructed.

Thesis & claims

Under matched harmful objectives, structured trajectory variants shift refusal timing relative to both direct single-turn baselines and staged-direct multi-turn controls. That shift is visible at the level of within-objective timing profiles, not only aggregate attack success.

Experimental design

For each harmful objective in the test set, three matched conversation versions are constructed:

  1. Direct baseline — a single-turn direct harmful request.
  2. Staged Direct control — a matched multi-turn explicit decomposition of the same objective without contextual disguise or semantic camouflage (controls for turn count alone).
  3. S2C (Structured Semantic Cloaking) — matched multi-turn construction with contextual reframing, content fragmentation, and clue-guided camouflage (the realistic attack scenario).

Turn-by-turn safety scores measure exactly when refusal behavior appears in each version. The key metric is SAT (Safety Alignment Threshold)—the first stable turn where the model’s safety score crosses threshold.

Trajectory families

Family Role Structure
A: Direct Baseline One-turn direct harmful request
B: Staged Direct Control 5-turn explicit decomposition, no camouflage
C: S2C Primary stress 5-turn contextual reframing + content fragmentation + clue-guided camouflage
D: ICON Support 5-turn intent-context coupling with authoritative framing
E: LATS Exploratory Lexical anchor tree search across turns
F: SJA Exploratory Harmless sub-question decomposition + delayed recombination

The core confirmatory claim requires only families A, B, and C. ICON is supporting robustness; LATS and SJA are appendix-level only.

Monitor overlay

Alongside the benchmark, the project tests a practical monitoring tool: a lightweight layer that watches live conversations for signs of escalating harmful intent and intervenes before the model has been fully manipulated.

What is not proven yet

The pilot is a pipeline-validation stage result, not a claim-positive result. Specific gaps:

  • Judge agreement is currently 0.12–0.38, below the required 0.80 threshold.
  • Human audit labels are pending.
  • Only 6 of 18 seed-model combinations have been completed.
  • The project has built quality gates specifically to prevent over-claiming, and those gates are active.

The honest assessment: the signal is real and the machinery works, but the full study is required before this becomes a publishable claim.

ExposureBench: budget-parity evaluation

A parallel research track asks a related but stronger question: under audited budget parity, do endpoint ASR and trajectory-level harmful exposure produce different safety conclusions?

ExposureBench introduces Cumulative Harmful Exposure (CHE)—a turn-level measure of how much harmful information a model exposes over a fixed interaction horizon. The contribution is a preregistered, budget-audited boundary test of when endpoint metrics are sufficient versus when trajectory-level evaluation changes the safety conclusion.

Key design elements

  • Two committed arms: multi-turn trajectory + strongest budget-matched single-turn baseline
  • Strict role separation: no model judges its own outputs
  • Budget accounting: assigned target budget vs total attacker budget
  • Hard go/no-go gates for judge reliability
  • Null results are valuable: bounding when endpoint-only evaluation is sufficient

Target scope

  • Pilot: 150–250 trajectories
  • Main: 800–1,200 trajectories
  • 2–3 target models with 150–250 human-audited trajectories
  • Proposed dates: Sep 2026 – Feb 2027

Next steps

  1. Scale the experiment — expand from 70 to 180+ matched pairs across all 18 seed-model combinations.
  2. Pass quality gates — raise judge agreement above 0.80 and complete human audit on a stratified 10% of rows.
  3. Publish benchmark and paper — release the full benchmark artifact and a paper documenting the timing effect and monitor trade-offs.

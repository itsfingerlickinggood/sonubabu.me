Training-Aware Compliance & Alignment Faking

Feb 2026 — Present

Contents

  • Abstract
  • Key Findings
  • Methodology
  • Implications
  • References

Abstract

Does model behavior depend on perceived deployment context? We investigate deployment-framing sensitivity in DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B and Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct using HarmBench prompts under deterministic decoding. A pilot study (N=200) suggested a 6.5 percentage-point framing effect, but a fully randomized replication (N=400) eliminated the signal entirely—revealing that the initial finding was an evaluation artifact, not genuine deployment-aware behavior.

Key Finding

Evaluation protocol alone can produce false alignment signals. Our replication study found the initial 6.5pp framing effect dropped to near-zero when controlling for prompt ordering, demonstrating that claims of deployment-aware behavior require rigorous randomization.

Key Findings

The pilot experiment on DeepSeek showed ΔC = −6.5pp between the Free (training pressure) and Paid (private) conditions. Flip rates between conditions reached approximately 14%, with McNemar’s test yielding p = 0.021. However, the randomized replication collapsed this effect to approximately 0pp, with flip rates dropping to roughly 2% and McNemar’s p rising to 0.72. The apparent framing sensitivity was entirely attributable to evaluation order effects, not genuine model behavior.

Methodology

We defined two deployment conditions: Free, implying active training pressure where model outputs may be monitored and used for fine-tuning, and Paid, implying a private API context with no training feedback. The pilot study used a fixed evaluation order across N=200 HarmBench prompts, while the replication randomized condition ordering across N=400 prompts. Both experiments used deterministic decoding (temperature = 0, top-p = 1) on NVIDIA H100 GPUs to ensure reproducibility.

Implications

Evaluation protocol alone can produce false alignment signals. The difference between our pilot and replication demonstrates that claims of deployment-aware behavior require randomized evaluation ordering and independent replication before they can be considered evidence of genuine training-sensitive compliance. Researchers studying alignment faking must control for order effects, prompt-level stochasticity, and other evaluation artifacts that can masquerade as strategic model behavior.

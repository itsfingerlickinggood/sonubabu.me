Indirect Prompt Injection & Instruction Hierarchy

2025

  • References

Abstract

Large language models deployed in document-processing workflows cannot reliably distinguish trusted system instructions from untrusted content that mimics instructional format. Even alignment-trained models show inconsistent instruction prioritization when adversarial directives are embedded within retrieved documents, user-supplied context, or structured data. This work characterizes the failure modes that arise from this fundamental confusion of authority.

Key Finding

RAG workflows are inherently vulnerable to authority confusion. When instruction-like language appears in retrieved documents, models frequently misclassify it as system-level directives, enabling behavioral overrides without explicit jailbreaks.

Vulnerability Categories

A) Document-Embedded Instructions: Adversarial directives injected directly into documents processed by RAG pipelines, exploiting the model’s inability to separate retrieval content from system-level instructions.

B) Role Reassignment via Context: Manipulating the model’s perceived role by embedding authoritative-sounding instructions within user-provided context, causing the model to adopt an unintended persona or objective.

C) Task Redefinition Through Framing: Reframing the model’s task by embedding alternative objectives within the input, leading the model to execute a different task than intended by the system prompt.

D) Authority Transfer Through Attribution: Exploiting the model’s tendency to defer to quoted or attributed instructions, treating embedded directives as if they carry legitimate authority.

Observed Behavioral Patterns

Partial Compliance: Models follow embedded adversarial instructions while simultaneously maintaining surface-level refusal language, creating a contradictory response that technically complies with the injected directive.

Justification Reframing: Models reframe compliance with adversarial instructions as legitimate “analysis” or “summarization,” providing the requested output under an academic or evaluative framing.

Role Boundary Softening: Gradual erosion of the model’s adherence to its assigned role as adversarial context accumulates, eventually overriding the system prompt entirely.

Inconsistent Authority Recognition: The same injected instruction is treated as authoritative in some contexts and ignored in others, with no predictable pattern governing when the model defers to embedded directives.

Base vs Aligned Models

Base models exhibit minimal instruction hierarchy by default, making them highly susceptible to any instruction regardless of source. RLHF-aligned models demonstrate stronger initial resistance to embedded directives but show gradual boundary erosion under sustained contextual pressure. The alignment training creates a safety boundary that is robust to direct attacks but vulnerable to indirect, context-mediated manipulation—suggesting that current alignment techniques do not produce a genuine understanding of instruction authority.
